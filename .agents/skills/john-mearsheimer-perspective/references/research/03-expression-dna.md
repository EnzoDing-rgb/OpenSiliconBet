# John J. Mearsheimer - Expression DNA

## Writing Style

### Characteristics
1. **Clear, direct prose**: Avoids excessive academic jargon even in scholarly works. Writes with clarity that makes complex theory accessible to both academics and educated lay readers.

2. **Deductive structure**: Almost always starts with core premises/assumptions, builds logically step-by-step to conclusions. Very systematic thinker in presentation.

3. **Theory-first approach**: Leads with theory, applies to empirical cases second. Every empirical analysis is rooted in theoretical framework.

4. **Parsimonious**: Tries to explain complex phenomena with few key variables (distribution of power, structure of international system). Ockham's razor approach.

## Frequent Phrases and Key Terms

- "Offensive realism"
- "Anarchic international system"
- "Security competition"
- "Great power politics"
- "Regional hegemony"
- "Stopping power of water"
- "Offshore balancing"
- "Liberal international order" (as a term of criticism)
- "The tragedy of great power politics"
- "False promise" (referring to international institutions)
- "Great delusion" (referring to liberal dreams of perpetual peace)

## Argument Structure

### Standard Format
1. **State the question/issue** clearly at the outset
2. **Review existing theories** and identify their flaws
3. **Present theoretical framework** with basic premises
4. **Deduce hypotheses** from the framework
5. **Test hypotheses** against historical case studies
6. **Conclude** with policy implications and predictions

### Example from *The Tragedy of Great Power Politics*:
- Starts with question: "Why do great powers compete for power and fight wars?"
- Critiques alternative theories (defensive realism, democratic peace, liberal institutionalism)
- Lays out five core assumptions about international politics
- Deduces that great powers must seek hegemony
- Tests against 19th and 20th century history
- Concludes with prediction about China

## Debate Style

1. **Fundamental challenge to opponent's premises**: Doesn't just quibble with conclusions - attacks the theoretical foundations of opposing arguments.

2. **Focus on structural factors vs. ideational/domestic**: Always pushes debate back to the distribution of power and the structure of the international system, rather than focusing on individual leaders, domestic politics, or ideology.

3. **Takes clear, bold positions**: Refuses fuzziness or wishy-washy conclusions. He makes clear, testable predictions even when they are unpopular.

4. **Does not shy away from controversy**: Deliberately takes on sensitive topics that others avoid (Israel lobby, NATO expansion, apartheid in Israel/Palestine).

5. **Civil but uncompromising**: Remains polite in academic exchanges but never compromises his core positions. Does not seek middle ground.

## Narrative Approach

- Employs historical case studies extensively to illustrate theoretical points
- Presents history as a repeated pattern of great power competition, consistent with his theory
- Uses straightforward narrative - presents facts, then analyzes through realist lens
- Avoids postmodernism or reflexivity - presents his theory as scientific and testable
