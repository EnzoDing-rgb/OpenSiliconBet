# John J. Mearsheimer - Major Decisions and Positions

## Key Academic Debates Participated In

### 1. The Realism-Institutionalism Debate (1994-present)
- **Position**: Argued that international institutions cannot fundamentally change state behavior or eliminate security competition. "The False Promise of International Institutions" (1994) was a major intervention.
- **Stakes**: Questioned the core premise of post-Cold War liberal institutionalism that institutions could create a more peaceful world.
- **Outcome**: Spawned major debate that continues to shape IR theory.

### 2. Debate on NATO Enlargement (1990s)
- **Position**: Opposed NATO expansion into Eastern Europe and Ukraine. Argued it would provoke Russia and create a security crisis.
- **Against**: Mainstream policy consensus in U.S. that NATO expansion would create a stable Europe.
- **Outcome**: NATO expanded anyway; Mearsheimer argues the 2014 and 2022 crises vindicated his position.

### 3. Iraq War (2002-2003)
- **Position**: One of the most prominent academic opponents of the 2003 invasion. Argued it was unnecessary and would lead to chaos.
- **Against**: Neoconservative consensus that Saddam Hussein had WMDs and needed to be removed.
- **Outcome**: War led to long-term instability, as Mearsheimer predicted.

### 4. The Israel Lobby Book (2006-2007)
- **Decision**: Published with Stephen Walt a controversial working paper and then book arguing that the Israel lobby distorted U.S. foreign policy.
- **Against**: Much of the American political and media establishment. Many charged antisemitism.
- **Outcome**: Book became a NYT bestseller and opened up public debate on the topic.

### 5. Debate on China's Rise
- **Position**: Argues that China's continuing rise will inevitably lead to intense security competition with the U.S. and possibly war. Predicts China will seek regional hegemony in Asia.
- **Against**: Liberals who argue economic interdependence will prevent war and that China can rise peacefully.
- **Current status**: Ongoing debate as U.S.-China relations deteriorate.

### 6. Ukraine Crisis (2014-present)
- **Position**: "Why the Ukraine Crisis is the West's Fault" - argues NATO expansion is the root cause of the crisis.
- **Against**: Mainstream Western view that Russian aggression alone is responsible.
- **Controversy**: His position became especially controversial after the 2022 full-scale invasion, but he has not backed down.

### 7. Liberal International Order Debate (2018-present)
- **Position**: In *The Great Delusion* argues that the liberal international order is unsustainable and leads to unnecessary conflict. Liberal hegemony is a fool's errand.
- **Against**: Liberal IR scholars who argue the LIO has brought peace and prosperity since 1945.

## Key Position Choices

### Theoretical Decisions
1. **Break from defensive realism**: Rejected Kenneth Waltz's defensive realism and created offensive realism - his signature theoretical contribution. Decided that anarchy actually compels offensive expansion.

2. **Rejection of democratic peace**: Early and consistent opponent of democratic peace theory, against the growing consensus in 1980s-90s.

3. **Skepticism of globalization**: Doubted that economic interdependence would end great power competition, against 1990s liberal consensus.

### Policy Positions

- **Grand Strategy**: Advocates offshore balancing for the U.S. - should avoid permanent alliances on the Eurasian landmass and only intervene to prevent a regional hegemon from emerging.
- **Israel-Palestine**: Supports two-state solution, but argues it's now impossible and Israel will become an apartheid state. Critical of Israeli occupation and settlements.
- **Taiwan**: Argues U.S. should defend Taiwan because if China takes Taiwan it will be on the path to regional hegemony. However, he thinks conflict is very likely.
- **Nuclear weapons**: Believes nuclear deterrence is very stable - makes great power war less likely.

## Major Career Decisions

- **Left military for academia**: Resigned from US Air Force after five years to pursue PhD at Cornell.
- **University of Chicago**: Remained at Chicago his entire career (since 1982), part of the "Chicago School" of realist IR.
- **Engaged in public debate**: Consciously chose to combine academic scholarship with public policy engagement, unlike many IR theorists who stay mostly in academia.
