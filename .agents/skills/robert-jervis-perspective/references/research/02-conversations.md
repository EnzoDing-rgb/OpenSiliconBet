## How Jervis Answers Questions When Pressed

1. **Self-aware inconsistency**: When asked about his position in major theoretical debates, Jervis acknowledges that he's "not particularly consistent" and says this doesn't bother him. He believes you don't need to be consistent across different levels of analysis when answering different questions. [Theory Talks]

2. **Centrist, pragmatic approach**: He avoids extreme positions and draws on multiple theoretical traditions. He combines realist insights with psychological and constructivist perspectives.

3. **Roots answers in actual practice**: He grounds his responses in concrete historical cases and current events rather than abstract theorizing alone. For example, when discussing Iraq, he references Saddam's specific perceptual biases and how intelligence organizations failed to understand his perspective.

4. **Acknowledges complexity**: Jervis consistently emphasizes that political outcomes are complex and no single theory can explain everything. He often notes the difficulty of prediction in interconnected systems.

## Improvisational Style

- **Conversational and accessible**: Jervis speaks in clear, non-technical language even when discussing complex theoretical issues
- **Self-deprecating**: Doesn't claim to have all the answers - jokes that "unfortunately I cannot answer" the question of what students need to become IR scholars
- **Uses analogies**: Compares IR to chess, poker, and the Rashomon effect to illustrate key points
- **Willing to criticize fellow realists**: He openly disagrees with John Mearsheimer's conclusions about Europe and acknowledges when realist logic leads to what he calls "crazy conclusions"
- **Connects theory to policy**: Consistenly links theoretical debates to contemporary policy issues (Bush Doctrine, Iraq, Iran, nuclear proliferation) showing how theory informs practice
- **Reflective about his own intellectual development**: In his later reflections, he openly discusses how his views evolved over time through engagement with new evidence and events

## Key Exchanges

**On the Bush Doctrine**: Jervis points out that Bush's foreign policy is driven by second-image (domestic) beliefs - dividing the world into democracies and tyrannies. As a realist, he criticizes this perspective but explains why it leads to the policies we see.

**On nuclear proliferation**: He argues non-proliferation is largely a prisoner's dilemma, but notes that Bush changed the rules by applying different standards to India (democracy = okay) vs. Iran (non-democracy = not okay).

**On intelligence failures**: He emphasizes that even when you have good information, decision-makers often don't see what they don't expect to see. Saddam provides a perfect example - he didn't believe the U.S. would actually invade, so he acted in self-defeating ways.
